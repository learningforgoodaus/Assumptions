Learning Activity: Understanding Assumptions

Select the chat bubbles to reveal information.

Common Thought

"They’re just bad at time management."

The Problem

This assumption overlooks possible underlying issues like DFV. Judging someone as lazy or disorganised can prevent us from noticing signs of stress, fear, or control.

Common Thought

"They’re taking advantage of flexible work policies."

The Problem

Bias can lead us to assume misuse rather than recognising a genuine need for safety or support. This mindset can discourage disclosure and harm trust.

Common Thought

"Their partner calls all the time because they care."

The Problem

Frequent calls may indicate monitoring or control, not affection. Assuming it’s harmless can make us miss coercive behaviours.

Common Thought

"They fight all the time—they’re just a toxic couple."

The Problem

This assumption minimises abuse and frames it as mutual conflict. It ignores power dynamics and the risk of escalating violence.

Common Thought

"If it was really bad, they’d leave."

The Problem

Leaving is often the most dangerous time for a victim. Assuming it’s easy to leave shows a lack of understanding of fear, control, and systemic barriers.

Common Thought

"That’s just how things are in their culture."

The Problem

This stereotype excuses violence and perpetuates discrimination. Abuse is never acceptable, regardless of cultural context.

The Goal: Awareness, Not Judgment

This isn't about telling you your values are wrong. The goal is self-awareness.

By understanding our own biases, we can make sure they don't stop us from acting when someone might need help.
